Clinical Pathophysiology
How P&S Dysfunction Explains Symptoms

Central Mechanism: Poor Cerebral Perfusion

PE & SW lead to inadequate brain blood flow, causing the brain to be "half asleep" during the day when upright.

Symptom Cascade

Neuropathy & Pain

Peripheral Autonomic Neuropathy, including Small Fiber Disorder (SFD)

MDD-like, ADD-like, Narcolepsy-like Symptoms

Result from chronic poor cerebral perfusion and PE & SW dysfunction

Hypothyroid Symptoms

Parasympathetics control hormone coordination; PE may induce hypothyroidism

Asthma-like Symptoms

Parasympathetics control immune system; Mast Cells release histamine causing airway inflammation

Hypertension

SE elevates BP as compensatory mechanism to support cerebral perfusion

Clinical Symptom Patterns
Understanding Daily Symptom Manifestations

Daytime (Upright Position)

  • Brain marginally perfused → "half asleep"
  • Depression-like symptoms
  • Headache or migraine
  • Brain fog and cognitive difficulties
  • Lightheadedness and fatigue
  • Memory and sensory disorders
  • ADD/ADHD-like symptoms (chronic demand for more blood)

Nighttime (Supine Position)

  • Brain finally receives adequate blood
  • Brain "wakes up" and "wants to play"
  • Sleep difficulties and insomnia
  • Twitching and Myoclonus (seizure-like activity from poor perfusion)

Anxiety-like Episodes

Any further drop in cerebral perfusion triggers "adrenaline storms" - brain calling for more blood:

  • Shortness of breath (SOB)
  • Palpitations
  • Chest tightness

Triggers: Eating, emotions, hormonal changes, dehydration

Additional Pathophysiology
Inflammation & Sensory Abnormalities

Sympathetic Excess (SE) Effects

  • Amplifies inflammation
    • Inflammatory pain responses
    • Joint pain
    • Asthma-like symptoms
  • Promotes anxiety-like symptoms
  • Note: SE is compensatory and will normalize organically as PE & SW are relieved

Cranial Nerve & Sensory Issues

  • Marginal brain perfusion → Cranial nerves marginally perfused → Abnormal behavior
  • SE-related inflammation may compress cranial nerves as they pass through skull
  • Result: Vision abnormalities and other sensory disturbances

Compensatory Mechanism Insight

SE (elevated BP) is the body's attempt to counter poor perfusion from PE & SW. Treating the root cause (PE & SW) allows SE to resolve naturally.

Treatment Strategy
Low & Slow Approach to P&S Rebalancing

Core Principle: Low-and-Slow

All P&S therapy must be low-dose and gradual to prevent additional symptoms. Due to ongoing stress (internal/external), expect slow relief with frequent setbacks and flares.

Treatment Goals

Primary Targets

  1. Relieve PE (Parasympathetic Excess)
  2. Treat SW (Sympathetic Withdrawal)
  3. SE resolves organically as PE & SW improve

Expected Outcomes

  • Improved cerebral perfusion
  • Symptom relief
  • Possible weaning from additional medications
  • Long-term autonomic balance

Long-term Outlook

The autonomic system has memory and can maintain balance once normalized. However, continued exposure to stressors requires longer-term therapy, preferably natural therapies only.

Pharmacological Treatment
Medication Recommendations

For PE (Parasympathetic Excess)

Nortriptyline
  • Dose: No more than 10mg once daily
  • Timing: 12 hours before waking, but before 8 PM
  • Mechanism: Acts as anti-cholinergic (not anti-depressant at this dose)
  • Benefits:
    • Helps pattern sleep
    • May help with headache and pain
    • Patients often self-wean from other antidepressants/anxiolytics
  • Note: Consult prescribing physician before weaning other psych meds

For SW (Sympathetic Withdrawal) & Abnormal Stand Response

  1. Hydration & Electrolytes: 6-8 glasses water daily + 1 tablespoon salt in 64 oz water (5.5g Sodium)
  2. Compression garments: Calf, thigh, abdomen (higher is better) when standing (not during exercise)
  3. r-Alpha-Lipoic Acid (ALA): ¼ tsp in water, once daily (Geronova brand) - nerve-selective antioxidant
  4. Midodrine: 2.5mg TID, titrated slowly from ½ pill once morning, over 12 weeks (6 increments)

Note: Low-dose Midodrine may raise BP a few mmHg; ALA will lower BP a few mmHg

Low & Slow Exercise Protocol
Retraining the Autonomic Nervous System

Critical Concept

With PE, all stress is perceived as unhealthy stress, even moderate exercise. The heart acts as if deconditioned due to P&S dysfunction. Goal: retrain nervous system to recognize manageable stress.

Exercise Requirements

  • Duration: 6-9 months minimum
  • Frequency: Daily (skipping 1 week restarts program)
  • Length: 40 contiguous minutes (may work up to this)
  • Intensity: Zero impact, pure cardiac workout
  • Target: No specific HR/BP - should converse normally without being winded after 40 min

✅ Recommended Activities

  • Walking: 2 mph maximum
  • Pool walking: Water above belly button, 2 mph pace
  • Easy biking: Pedal like walking at 2 mph
  • Swimming: NOT arms + legs together (use kickboard OR arms only, very slow)

❌ Avoid

  • Elliptical (HR rises too fast)
  • Standard swimming (too many muscles)
  • Any activity raising HR quickly
  • Activities causing tissue breakdown
  • Forcing through fatigue/pain
Exercise Protocol Details
Critical Guidelines & Modifications

Why Low & Slow Works

Patient loves exercise because whole body helps heart pump blood to brain. However, post-exercise malaise is the problem. Low & slow introduces manageable stress to break the "bad habit" of everything being perceived as stress.

Stopping Criteria

Patient determines true limit: If fatigue or pain requires forcing through, STOP IMMEDIATELY. That's when healthy exercise becomes bad stress. Duration doesn't matter.

Minimal Exercise Option (High Stress Days)

When even basic exercise is too strenuous:

  • Position: Lie supine (on back)
  • Activity: Kick lower legs as if walking at 2 mph
  • Purpose: Move blood from feet to heart without fighting gravity
  • Benefit: Delivers blood to brain, making exercise more tolerable

Aqua Therapy Prescription

Consider prescribing: Aqua Therapy, 3 days/week

  • May help insurance cover pool membership
  • Actual therapist not needed (their "more is better" contradicts P&S needs)
  • Swimming most recommended for autonomic patients

Long-term Goal

After 6-9 months and normalized Parasympathetic activity, more strenuous exercise may be gradually returned. Parasympathetics (re-)trained to not over-react to stressful stimuli.
